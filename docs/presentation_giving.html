Giving a Presentation in Economics¶

Presume that you do not know how to give a good presentation

The Big 5 are also crucial for talks/presentations

The Big 5¶

Google and read The Big 5 and Other Ideas for Presentations by Donald Cox (available online)

Start with a title slide, mentioning your topic title and your name

  1. What are you doing?

    In 25 words or less, and in plain English, what is the hypothesis or research question that you are investigating?

  2. Why is it important?

    There are usually many reasons for why your topic is important. Just point to most important ones.

  3. How is the existing literature different?

    Tell us about the gaps in the literature and how you are trying to fill them

    Be gentle towards other papers

  4. What are you doing that’s better?

    What model are you using to answer the research question?

    What is your innovation/gap?

    New technique? Old technique in a new context? Little tweak?

  5. What did you find?

    Only provide main findings, first order effects

    For the purpose of the week 7 presentation, tell us what you expect to find; the type of result that you are looking for (I don’t mean that you should guess your results)

    Do not be too modest

    Do not be too aggressive

You could cover the Big 5 on as little as 5 slides, after which you can take a deeper dive into your research

Even research that is in the early stages can have a well-defined takeaway: What is the main thing you want your audience to remember about your talk when they leave? Whatever that thing is, tell it to them right now.

Other quick tips¶

A one hour talk should have about 25-35 slides

A one and a half our talk should have about 35-45 slides

Never ever prepare too many slides. Occasionally a speaker walks in with a slide show of, say, 75 slides with the plan to only cover a third of these. That’s an awful idea and usually ends badly.

Minimize jargon

Give specific examples

Avoid too much detail

Make your tables readable (font size, and layout!), explain them slowly to the audience

Don’t digress, don’t tell spontaneous anecdotes
